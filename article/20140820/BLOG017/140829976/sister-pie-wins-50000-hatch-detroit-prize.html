Hatch winner's pie persistence: A recipe with key ingredients

Lisa Ludwinski intends to open her bakery in Detroit's
West Village neighborhood

Amy Haimerl
Small business, entrepreneurship and the city of Detroit
  • Tweet
  • Share
  • Share
  • Email
  • More
    Reprints Print
    Amy Haimerl
    Lisa Ludwinski (center) with her sister (far left), mother and future kitchen manager Anji Reynolds (right).

    Welcome to the Hatch Detroit club, Sister Pie.

    Owner Lisa Ludwinski is the winner of the 2014 Hatch Detroit competition, which awards the victor $50,000 courtesy of Comerica Bank. Ludwinski will use the cash infusion to open her bakery, Sister Pie, in Detroit's West Village neighborhood on the corner of Kercheval and Parker streets.

    Those of you who follow my columns know that this is good news for me. I live down the block and can't wait for her planned opening of April 2015 so I can eat all the sour cherry bourbon pie I want. (Or that my Fitbit will allow.)

    But, honestly, I wasn't rooting just for her. I was rooting for all four finalists, including Third Wave Music (a full-service music shop trying to open in Midtown) and Cockadoodle (a breakfast and lunch joint planned for the Avenue of Fashion — that's Livernois and 7 Mile Road) and Gabriel Hall (a New Orleans-style restaurant/club/museum in Woodbridge). In the past week of voting, I cast my ballot for each of them, effectively canceling myself out. But I was excited to bring any of these new businesses into our fair city.

    I wasn't the only one. Hatch Detroit founder Nick Gorga told the sold-out crowd at Ford Field that "this was an exceptional year. These are four businesses that will be open within the year."

    And the Detroit Lions have stepped in to help these — and past finalists — get off the ground. After all, Hatch has gotten good at growing not just the winners but also an entire bench of retail business talent. Some of the most popular names in Detroit small business are alums of the program.

    Batch Brewing Co. won last year. Bachelor-lifestyle shop Hugh and tapas restaurant LaFeria are open just blocks from each other on Cass Avenue. Bra shop Busted in Detroit was a semifinalist last year and is open and thriving in the Park Shelton building. Detroit Vegan Soul is serving up tasty Southern fried tofu bites in the West Village, while the Detroit Institute of Bagels is satiating cravings over in Corktown.

    But of course, there can be only one. And the success of Ludwinski and Sister Pie speaks not just to the power of a bakery but to the power of a community.

    Ludwinski applied to Hatch last year, but Sister Pie didn't even make it to the semifinals. She just wasn't ready. Ludwinski learned from that, regrouped and really focused on building a strong business — not just an idea for a business. She worked with TechTown Detroit's SWOT City program and with FoodLab Detroit, a nonprofit member organization that guides fledgling food businesses from startup through growth.
    Lisa Ludwinski

    That effort showed when Ludwinski took the stage for the so-called "Hatch Off," in which a panel of judges grills the finalists about their business plans. At this point, it's no longer about popularity or dream or idea. That's all for the community voting phase. Now the judges — fellow business owners and nonprofit leaders — must evaluate the finalists on their business acumen to get a sense of their likelihood of success.

    That is where Ludwinski shined. FoodLab co-directors Jess Daniel and Devita Davison prepped her for her presentation. They helped her refine her answers and tell a story of what it would take to succeed not just as a bakery but as a small-business owner.

    The other competitors were strong and showed real promise, but Ludwinski offered precise, numbers-based responses overlaid with humor and community spirit. It was a winning combination for the judges, who were unanimous in their decision.

    When judge Hugh Yaro, owner of the restaurant CraftWork, asked the finalists about their three- to five-year plan, Ludwinski responded that she saw steady growth in her wholesale business. She would be in local supermarkets and restaurants and planned to start a pie workshop program similar to Zingerman's classes. She had a precise plan for when the bakery would be open and a deposit on the space.

    She was able to answer questions from Avalon International Bread's Jackie Victor about local sourcing, telling her that she already had a "plum guy and a maple syrup guy" at Eastern Market and that she had plans in place for how to get better prices from local wholesalers so she could stay focused on local sourcing.

    She was able to directly answer questions about overhead from Kirk Mayes, the new CEO of Forgotten Harvest, telling him that payroll would be her biggest budget suck. She anticipates a monthly operating budget of $8,500, most of it in salaries. The more she expands, however, the more she'll need to hire.

    "Pie is a labor of love," she told Mayes.

    Finally, Lydia Gutierrez, CEO of Hacienda Mexican Foods, asked the contestants to fill in the blanks in this statement: My business is not about (blank) but it is about (blank). Ludwinski was the last competitor to answer, so she had a bit more time to think on her feet, and this was her response:

    "My business is not about me, but it is about my employees, neighbors and community."

    Her presentation was so strong, in fact, that some crowd members questioned whether the level of competition in Hatch has grown beyond the intent. If businesses are this sophisticated, they pondered, shouldn't they be able to access capital in the traditional methods? Is a program like Hatch intended to help those who can't get funding to actually open their doors? Or is it to push forward the best retail idea and give them a shot the arm, regardless of need?

    It's a question for Gorga and the Hatch Detroit team to ponder for the future. In the meantime, Ludwinski is just ecstatic that this was her year.

    "I'm so excited," she said. "I'm in shock. But I've had a lot of help and support throughout. I'm so grateful to have an opportunity to be in this great contest."

    See you round the way for some pie very soon, I hope.

    And to the other finalists — and semifinalists — I can't wait to see you open, too. After all, Busted didn't make it to the finals last year and the bra shop is already open and thriving in Detroit's Midtown.
