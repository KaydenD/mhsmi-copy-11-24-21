NOVEMBER IS BLACK CATHOLIC HISTORY MONTH
There is a cause for celebration of Black people in the Catholic church! According to the U.S. Conference of Catholic Bishops, there are over 3 million Black Catholics in the US. and 798 predominantly African American parishes. “While many [B]lack people are Baptist, the second-largest religious group that [B]lack people belong to in the U.S. is the Catholic Church. There are 250 African American priests, 437 deacons, 75 men of African descent in seminary formation, 400 African American religious sisters, and 50 religious brothers. These numbers do not include missionaries from Africa who now serve in various ministries throughout the United States” (Catholic Extension). 

Another reason to celebrate is the 5 Black men and women who are being considered for canonization for their inspiring lives:

  1. Venerable Pierre Toussaint (1766-1853), the former slave and philanthropist who is buried in New York’s Saint Patrick’s Cathedral
  2. Venerable Henriette Delille (1813-1862), from New Orleans, a descendant of slaves who founded the Sisters of the Holy Family
  3. Venerable Augustus Tolton (1854-1897), who was born in slavery and eventually attended seminary in Rome because no American seminary would accept him, and who was a pastor in Chicago
  4. Servant of God Mother Mary Elizabeth Lange (ca. 1794-1882), the Haitian refugee who had a passion for educating children and who founded the Oblate Sisters of Providence
  5. Servant of God Julia Greeley (c. 1833-1918), the former slave who was Denver’s “Angel of Mercy,” now buried in Denver’s cathedral

CREATING & MAINTAINING INCLUSIVE CLASSROOMS: INCLUDING NATIVE AMERICANS IN OUR CURRICULUM
As we approach Thanksgiving and Native American Heritage History month, consider including Native American materials in your lesson planning. I have included some resources and best practices to do so. See the resources below and consider utilizing them this month and beyond.

“We Are Still Here”: An Interview with Debbie Reese -best practices for selecting and teaching literature on Native Americans

Teaching Thanksgiving in a Socially Responsible Way

Thanksgiving Mourning - In this activity, students will explore the perspectives of two Native American authors about the meaning of the Thanksgiving holiday and then write journal entries.

Pre-Columbian Native Peoples and Technology - The purpose of this lesson is for students to grapple with three separate definitions: primitive, civilized (civilization), and technology. Students examine or re-examine their definitions of these words and how these words define what they understand about Pre-Columbian native culture. The objective is to help students determine their point of view.

Stereotypes and Tonto - This lesson revolves around Sherman Alexie’s poignant yet humorous and accessible essay, “I Hated Tonto (Still Do).” It explores the negative impact that stereotypes have on the self-worth of individuals and the damage that these stereotypes inflict on pride in one’s heritage. The reading is supported by a short video montage of clips from Western films. The clips offer students the opportunity to evaluate primary sources for bias and bigotry, as well as providing context for the protagonists’ experiences in the essay.

NORMS FOR HAVING DIFFICULT CONVERSATIONS
Consider these norms from other Sisters of Mercy schools as you have conversations around DEI issues.

From Mount St. Mary Academy-Watchung, NJ:
  • Speak from the “I” perspective
  • Be fully present
  • Be self-responsible and self-challenging
  • Listen to understand
  • Accept respectful conflict as a necessary catalyst for change
  • Suspend judgment
  • Be comfortable with silence
  • Make mistakes and let go
  • Lean into discomfort
  • Respect confidentiality-Respect candidness of others as a gift

From Mercy High School Baltimore’s civil discourse norms:
  • Maintain engagement
  • Activate intentional listening skills
  • Inquire further when confronted with misunderstandings or disagreements
  • Cultivate an open mind
